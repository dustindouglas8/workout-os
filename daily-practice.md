# Daily Practice System

This document is the complete specification of the morning rehab/prehab session and night recovery routine. It covers the full day architecture outside the main training session: morning sequence, fixed circuit (F1–F4), rotating movements (R1–R2), ankle stiffness block, ab vacuum protocol, and night routine. The immediate workout warm-up lives in `screens.md` and is generated from the day's main session.

---

## 1. Morning Sequence — Exact Daily Order with Timing

The morning sequence is a fixed-order protocol. Steps do not move. Nothing is added between steps. Total time from wake to training start: approximately 25–35 minutes.

```
Wake
│
├── [2 min] Nasal breathing
│   └── Remain supine or seated. Inhale through nose only, 4s in / 6s out.
│       Purpose: activate CO2 tolerance, shift from sympathetic wake state
│       to parasympathetic baseline before adding any demand.
│
├── [3 min] Ab vacuum × 3 reps
│   └── Fasted, before any food or liquid.
│       Phase-appropriate position (see Section 5).
│       Hold per phase target. Full exhale before each vacuum.
│
├── [6 min] Morning circuit (F1–F4 + R1–R2)
│   └── 1 round, no rest, 50% effort ceiling.
│       See Section 2 (fixed) and Section 3 (rotating).
│
├── [4 min] Ankle stiffness block
│   └── Immediately after circuit, before any food.
│       See Section 4.
│
├── Matcha
│   └── Prepared and consumed here. Not before the circuit.
│       No food — matcha only. If adding L-theanine powder, mix here.
│
├── Pre-workout amino stack
│   └── 20–30 min before training begins.
│       Creatine + EAAs + electrolytes mixed in water.
│       Caffeine already delivered via matcha — do not double-dose.
│
└── TRAIN
    └── Session begins here later or immediately after.
        Readiness check and immediate warm-up happen inside the
        training session (see screens.md 4b-4c).
```

**Training day vs. rest day divergence:** On rest days, the sequence runs identically through the ankle stiffness block. After the ankle block on rest days: walk 20–30 min outside (Zone 2, conversational pace). No matcha timing constraint on rest days. Pre-workout stack is skipped.

## 2. Morning Circuit — Fixed Foundation (F1–F4)

These 4 movements appear every day, every phase, regardless of what muscles train today. They are not chosen for variety or enjoyment — they are chosen because each one addresses a structural system that, if neglected, becomes the limiting factor in the primary program. See philosophy.md Section 7 for the full rationale.

**Design rules:** 50% effort ceiling. No pause between movements. 1 round only. Total time: ~4 minutes.

---

### F1: Band External Rotation

**Primary target:** Infraspinatus, teres minor (posterior rotator cuff)
**Equipment:** Light resistance band anchored at elbow height

**Phase-by-phase reps:**
- Phase 1: 2 × 10/side, slow (2s out / 2s back)
- Phase 2: 2 × 12/side, controlled
- Phase 3: 2 × 15/side, 1s pause at external rotation peak

**Coaching cue:** Elbow locked at 90 degrees, pinned to side. The rotation happens at the shoulder only — the elbow does not drift forward. Do not squeeze at the end position; simply control the return. Think "open the door" outward, not "yank it."

**Why it is in the fixed rotation:** The rotator cuff is the primary shoulder joint stabilizer. At 225lb pressing volume (push sessions 2x/week), the cuff is the structural soft constraint. Daily band ER at low intensity maintains the eccentric strength that prevents shoulder impingement and labrum stress. When the cuff fails, the shoulder program fails — and cuff failure is silent and slow.

**Transfer to main session:** Every pressing movement (push-up, dip, ring push-up). Every pulling movement (pull-up, ring row). The cuff is active in both but rarely trained directly. This is the only direct cuff work in the program.

---

### F2: Hip Flexor Kneeling Stretch + Posterior Tilt

**Primary target:** Psoas major, iliacus (hip flexors), lumbar erectors secondarily
**Equipment:** Yoga mat or soft surface under back knee

**Phase-by-phase reps:**
- Phase 1: 30s hold/side, 1 posterior pelvic tilt at 15s mark
- Phase 2: 40s hold/side, 3 active posterior tilts distributed through hold
- Phase 3: 45s hold/side + 5 active knee drives (anterior compression at end range)

**Coaching cue:** Lunge position, back knee on ground. Before beginning the stretch, actively tuck the pelvis (posteriorly tilt — imagine pulling belt buckle toward chest). This engages the glute of the rear leg and prevents the pelvis from dumping forward into anterior tilt, which would reduce psoas stretch and load the lumbar spine. Breathe into the stretch; do not force it.

**Why it is in the fixed rotation:** Chronically shortened hip flexors drive anterior pelvic tilt, which compresses the lumbar spine (especially under load) and reduces glute activation during squats and hinges. Every lower body session, every pull-up, and every standing exercise is degraded by tight hip flexors. At a desk job, hip flexors shorten further overnight. This movement is the daily reset.

**Transfer to main session:** Squat depth (glutes activate more with neutral pelvis), hinge mechanics (lumbar safety), hollow body position in pull-ups (posterior pelvic tilt is the same motion — practiced daily here).

---

### F3: Dead Bug — Exhale Each Rep

**Primary target:** Transverse abdominis (TVA), diaphragm, lumbar stabilizers
**Equipment:** None (mat optional)

**Phase-by-phase reps:**
- Phase 1: 6 reps/side (1 rep = opposite arm + leg extension), slow exhale through each rep
- Phase 2: 8 reps/side, add 2s pause at full extension
- Phase 3: 10 reps/side, add band or light weight in extended arm (0.5–2kg)

**Coaching cue:** Supine, arms vertical above chest, knees at 90 degrees. Before moving, exhale fully and draw navel toward spine (this is TVA engagement — same contraction as the ab vacuum). Hold this engagement while extending opposite arm and leg. Do NOT let the lower back arch away from the floor — this is the rep standard. Breathe in on the return; exhale again before the next extension.

**Why it is in the fixed rotation:** TVA activation is the foundation of spinal stability in every loaded movement. The dead bug is chosen over crunches or planks because it trains TVA while the limbs move — which is the actual demand during pull-ups (legs swinging), presses (shoulder loading while core stabilizes), and squats (spinal load while hips move). The exhale-every-rep cue links it to the ab vacuum (same muscle, same contraction pattern).

**Transfer to main session:** Hollow body position in pull-ups, plank stability in push-ups, spinal bracing in all hinge movements. The dead bug is the practice rep for the core contraction required in every primary exercise.

---

### F4: Tibialis Raise + Single-Leg Calf Raise

**Primary target:** Tibialis anterior (tibialis raise), soleus + gastrocnemius (calf raise)
**Equipment:** Wall or doorframe for balance; elevated surface optional for calf raise

**Phase-by-phase reps:**
- Phase 1: Tibialis raise × 15 (bilateral, heels on ground, toes raise) + single-leg calf raise × 8/side (no elevation)
- Phase 2: Tibialis raise × 20 + single-leg calf raise × 10/side (slight elevation — book or step, +2–3 cm range)
- Phase 3: Tibialis raise × 25 + single-leg calf raise × 12/side (full step elevation, 3s lowering)

**Coaching cue — Tibialis raise:** Back against wall, heels ~6 inches from wall. Raise toes as high as possible, hold 1 second at top, lower with control. Do not rush. The tibialis anterior fatigues quickly — that is the correct stimulus.

**Coaching cue — Single-leg calf raise:** Full range of motion — heel drops below surface at bottom (if using elevation), full plantar flexion at top. Control the lowering. This is a single-leg movement because bilateral calf raises allow the stronger leg to compensate, masking asymmetry.

**Why it is in the fixed rotation:** The tibialis anterior is chronically underdeveloped in most athletes. It bears significant load at 225lb during all walking, jumping, and ankle-driven movements. Tibialis weakness contributes to patellar tendon stress (tibial rotation under load), shin splints, and ankle instability. Daily work at low intensity is more effective than periodic high-intensity work because connective tissue adapts slowly. This is the daily maintenance protocol for the lower kinetic chain.

**Transfer to main session:** Ankle stiffness block (directly), pogo hops, all lower body work. The tibialis raise is also the primary progression marker in the ankle benchmark test (Section 4).

## 3. Morning Circuit — Rotating Movements (R1–R2 by Day)

Two rotating movements are added after F1–F4 each day, bringing the total circuit to 6 exercises (the maximum). These movements are selected from the user's rehab/prehab needs and the day's training context — they organize the system without replacing the immediate warm-up before the main session.

**Note:** R1 and R2 serve different functions from the immediate warm-up. The morning versions run at a 50% effort ceiling and are general maintenance or low-load preparation. When a rotating movement is the same exercise as something in the immediate warm-up, the warm-up must treat the morning version as completed context — either skip the duplicate, reduce the dose, or replace it with a different drill that serves the same warm-up purpose. "May overlap when useful" means the morning primes a movement the session will use; it does not mean the same exercise should appear identically in both blocks.

Example: if the main session includes Jefferson curls, the morning rotation must not also include Jefferson curls (even loaded lightly) — the morning version turns loaded end-range spinal flexion into a triple exposure when the main session has it. Substitute with a decompression or spinal segmentation drill instead.

## 4. Ankle Stiffness Block

**Position in sequence:** Immediately after morning circuit. Before matcha. Before any food or drink other than water.

**Rationale:** Ankle stiffness is addressed first because tendinous tissue (Achilles, plantar fascia, patellar tendon) is most compliant and responsive to mechanical loading in the immediate post-sleep window before sustained loading begins. This block takes 4 minutes and is the daily investment in lower limb structural resilience. At 225lb, the Achilles-calcaneal system bears approximately 2.5–3x bodyweight with every step. Daily low-load pogo work is preventive medicine.

### Movements

**Pogo hops × 15 (training days only)**
- Bilateral, minimal ground contact time, stiff ankles (not heel-to-toe roll)
- Focus: Achilles-driven spring, not plantar flexion push
- Standard: heels do not touch ground; amplitude is small (2–4 inches)
- Rest days substitute: barefoot single-leg balance × 30s/side (eyes open Phase 1, eyes closed Phase 2+)

**Tibialis raise × 20**
- Default: completed in F4. Do not repeat in the morning ankle block.
- If tibialis is the explicit rehab priority for the day, add only a reduced supplemental dose here: tibialis raise × 12.
- If the main session also contains ankle stiffness work, the main session must show tibialis work as completed context.

**Single-leg soleus raise × 10/side**
- Default: completed in F4 as the single-leg calf raise. Do not repeat in the morning ankle block.
- If soleus/Achilles is the explicit rehab priority for the day, add only a reduced supplemental dose here: single-leg soleus raise × 6/side.
- Slow and controlled: 2s up, 2s hold at top, 3s lower.
- Knee slightly bent (~20–30°) to bias soleus over gastrocnemius.

**Total time:** 4 minutes. This block does not get skipped on busy mornings.

**Morning ownership rule:** In normal operation, F4 owns tibialis and calf/soleus maintenance. The morning ankle block owns elastic/proprioceptive work only: pogo hops on training days or barefoot single-leg balance on rest days. Tibialis and soleus may be repeated in the morning ankle block only when the day is explicitly marked as an ankle-priority rehab day, and the UI must label that dose as supplemental.

**Ankle block / main session ownership rule:** The main session may contain a separate Ankle Stiffness Block (pogo hops + tibialis raise + soleus raise). When the morning ankle block is completed, the main session must show it as completed context and omit duplicate tibialis raise and soleus raise. Pogo hops may be kept in the session if the day is lower-body or plyometric-dominant and a session-specific elastic primer is justified. Tibialis anterior and Achilles/soleus tissue must not be loaded in three separate blocks (F4, morning ankle block, main session) on the same day without explicit intent. Choose one: the morning ankle block owns the tibialis/soleus volume, or the main session owns it — not both.

## 5. Ab Vacuum Protocol

**Timing:** Fasted, immediately after waking, before the morning circuit. First thing. No exceptions — the fasted state is required for this to work as intended. Food in the stomach mechanically prevents the visceral draw.

**Mechanism:** The ab vacuum trains the transverse abdominis (TVA) through a concentric/isometric contraction that draws the viscera inward and upward. This is distinct from the TVA bracing trained in dead bugs — this is the active drawing-in (hollow body prerequisite) that reduces waist circumference over time through a combination of TVA hypertrophy and habitual neuromuscular control of visceral positioning.

### Position Progression by Phase

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1–5): Standing**
- Feet shoulder-width, slight forward lean, hands on knees
- Exhale completely (all air out)
- Draw navel toward spine and up toward ribcage
- Hold target: 10 seconds × 3 reps, full exhale between reps
- Easier to feel TVA engagement in this position; gravity-neutral for visceral draw

**Phase 2 (Weeks 6–10): All-Fours (Tabletop Position)**
- Hands and knees, spine neutral
- Same exhale → draw sequence
- Hold target: 20 seconds × 3 reps
- All-fours position allows gravity to assist visceral draw (organs fall away from spine)
- Harder to maintain than standing; scapular stability required to avoid collapsing

**Phase 3 (Weeks 11–16): Hanging (Dead Hang from Pull-Up Bar)**
- Full dead hang, both hands, relaxed grip focus
- Same exhale → draw sequence
- Hold target: 30 seconds × 3 reps
- Maximum gravity assistance (full body weight increases visceral draw force)
- Also trains grip endurance passively — pull-up bar hang during vacuum integrates two skills

### Connection to Pull-Up Mechanics

The ab vacuum (TVA concentric draw-in) and the hollow body position in pull-ups (TVA bracing with posterior pelvic tilt) use the same muscle group via different mechanisms. Vacuum trains the active "in and up" contraction. Dead bug trains the bracing-under-limb-load contraction. Pull-up hollow body requires both simultaneously. Daily vacuum + daily dead bug = progressive neuromuscular preparation for hollow body pull-up form, without requiring any time on the bar to achieve it.

## 6. Night Recovery — 7 Movements, 7 Minutes

**Purpose:** Parasympathetic activation and mechanical unloading after training and daily stress. The night routine is not an optional wind-down — it is a physiological state change. It shifts the nervous system from the sympathetic-dominant state required for training and work into the parasympathetic-dominant state required for quality sleep and growth hormone release.

**Label used in the app:** *"Night Recovery — 7 movements, 7 minutes. Not optional for recovery."*

**Design principle:** No movement in this sequence produces muscle damage, fatigue, or mechanical load. Everything is decompressing, releasing, or breathing. Total effort: 10–20% of max.

---

### Movement 1: Physiological Sigh × 3

**Duration:** ~1 minute
**What system it addresses:** CO2 dysregulation, acute stress, respiratory alkalosis from the training day

**Execution:** Double inhale through the nose (short sniff, then full inhale on top of it), followed by a very long, slow exhale through the mouth. The double inhale reinflates alveoli that have partially collapsed under stress. The prolonged exhale activates the vagus nerve and drops heart rate within 1–3 cycles.

**Coaching note:** This is the fastest known method to reduce acute physiological stress. 3 cycles takes ~90 seconds. Do it before any other movement. Do it lying down if possible.

---

### Movement 2: Passive Hip Flexor Stretch × 40s/side

**Duration:** 80 seconds (40s × 2 sides)
**What system it addresses:** Psoas/iliacus (same as F2, but now in passive mode — no active work)

**Execution:** Kneeling lunge position, back knee on mat. Release into the stretch — no active posterior tilt here. Let gravity do the work. This is passive, not active. Breathe into the front of the hip.

**Coaching note:** The morning F2 is active (posterior tilt engaged). This is the passive counterpart — releasing, not strengthening. Think of it as the end of the conversation with the hip flexor that the morning began. The psoas also attaches to the lumbar vertebrae — releasing it at night decompresses the lumbar spine before sleep.

---

### Movement 3: Supine Thoracic Release × 2 minutes

**Duration:** 2 minutes
**What system it addresses:** Thoracic spine extension, mid-back compression from desk posture and training

**Execution:** Lie on back, foam roller (or rolled towel) perpendicular to spine, positioned at mid-back (T6–T8 region). Arms crossed across chest or overhead (overhead increases stretch). Allow gravity to produce thoracic extension over the roller. Breathe slowly.

**Coaching note:** If no foam roller, use a rolled yoga mat or folded blanket. The goal is passive thoracic extension — the thoracic spine is chronically flexed from all anterior-dominant activity. 2 minutes of gravity-assisted extension significantly improves thoracic mobility over weeks, which in turn improves shoulder overhead position for pull-ups and presses.

---

### Movement 4: Exhale-Only Supine Reset × 6/side

**Duration:** ~2 minutes
**What system it addresses:** Diaphragm reset, CO2 regulation, parasympathetic shift. This is not TVA training. It uses the same body position as the morning dead bug but is a breath regulation tool, not a core exercise.

**Execution:** Supine, arms over chest, knees 90°. Extend opposite arm and leg ONLY on the exhale — no muscular loading, breath-paced only. On the inhale, return. Do not brace. Do not count reps as training volume. The movement exists to anchor the exhale and slow the nervous system.

**Coaching note:** Do not call this a "dead bug" in the app — that label carries training intent. This movement is labeled "Exhale-Only Supine Reset" and cued as recovery. If today included core accessory work (plank, hollow hold, hanging knee raise), this movement can be replaced with crocodile breathing (Movement 6) moved earlier in the sequence.

---

### Movement 5: Supine Twist × 30s/side

**Duration:** 60 seconds (30s × 2 sides)
**What system it addresses:** Lumbar rotation, thoracolumbar fascia, spinal decompression

**Execution:** Supine. Bring one knee to chest, cross it over the body with light hand pressure, extend the same-side arm out. Let the twisted position relax for 30 seconds. Breathe slowly; allow the lower back to settle into the position. Switch sides.

**Coaching note:** Do not force the knee toward the floor. The goal is spinal rotation, not hip ROM. If the opposite shoulder lifts off the floor, the rotation is sufficient. This movement addresses the cumulative spinal compression of the training day and returns fluid to intervertebral discs.

---

### Movement 6: Crocodile Breathing × 2 minutes

**Duration:** 2 minutes
**What system it addresses:** Diaphragm mechanics, residual sympathetic tone, CO2 tolerance

**Execution:** Prone (face down), arms folded under forehead. Breathe slowly and fully into the lower back and sides — not the chest. Exhale completely. The focus is on feeling the lower back and ribs expand laterally on each inhale. Slow: aim for 4–6 breaths per minute.

**Coaching note:** This position trains diaphragmatic breathing by mechanically preventing chest breathing (face-down, chest compressed by the floor). The result is a direct training stimulus for the diaphragm's lateral and posterior expansion, which is the most efficient breathing pattern and the most parasympathetic-activating. 2 minutes of this after a training day significantly reduces resting heart rate before sleep.

---

### Movement 7: 4-7-8 Breath × 4–5 Cycles

**Duration:** ~2 minutes (4–5 cycles × ~20s each)
**What system it addresses:** Autonomic nervous system regulation, cortisol clearance, sleep preparation

**Execution:** Supine or seated. Inhale through nose for 4 seconds. Hold for 7 seconds. Exhale through mouth for 8 seconds (audible exhale — let it release). Repeat 4–5 cycles.

**Coaching note:** The 4-7-8 protocol was developed by Dr. Andrew Weil from pranayama techniques. The extended hold forces CO2 accumulation, which triggers the parasympathetic response via peripheral chemoreceptor signaling. The 8-second exhale activates the vagus nerve. After 4–5 cycles, most athletes report a distinct physical shift — body temperature may drop slightly, muscle tension releases, jaw relaxes. This is the target state. This is the last movement before sleep.

## 7. Day-Type Morning Framing

The morning sequence is physically identical on training days and rest days. The intent is different.

**Training day morning intent:** "Organize the system for work. The session requires a prepared substrate — CNS, joints, TVA, and ankles must be ready to receive load."

**Rest day morning intent:** "Maintain the system without adding load. The morning circuit runs at the same effort ceiling but the goal is circulation and movement quality, not preparation for a session. The ankle block substitutes balance for hops. After the circuit, walk."

This distinction should be surfaced in the app's morning framing copy. The morning routine is not about "getting ready to work out." On rest days it serves an entirely different purpose — active recovery signaling, connective tissue maintenance, and habit reinforcement. The same 15-minute sequence serves both purposes; only the framing changes.

## 8. The 4 Rules — Morning Circuit

These four rules are invariant. They govern the morning circuit design and prevent scope creep, intensity creep, and drift from the original intent. They apply regardless of how the user feels that morning.

**Rule 1: 6 exercises maximum**
The circuit occupies 4 fixed slots (F1–F4) and 2 rotating slots (R1–R2). No seventh exercise is ever added. When users want to add something, it belongs in the immediate warm-up or main session, not in the morning rehab/prehab circuit.

**Rule 2: 1 round only**
No second round. The temptation to repeat the circuit — especially on high-energy mornings — is a sign the circuit is being treated as training rather than organization. A second round pushes it into warm-up territory and competes with the main session.

**Rule 3: No rest between movements**
The circuit flows continuously, one movement to the next. Pausing to catch breath or adjust equipment is acceptable; programmed rest intervals are not. If rest is needed between movements, effort is too high — reduce to 50%.

**Rule 4: 50% effort ceiling**
This is the only effort regulation instruction in the morning sequence. It means: if 50% effort on band ER is 15 reps, do 15 reps. If the tibialis raise at 50% feels easy at 15 reps, do 15 reps and stop. Do not add reps, do not slow down to "get more out of it." The ceiling prevents the morning circuit from becoming a training session.
