# Dustin v4 Nutrition System

**Purpose:** Complete nutrition system extracted from the Dustin v4 program. Serves as the master template for generating individualized nutrition configs. Sections 1–9 reflect the Dustin v4 implementation. Section 10 defines the parametric variables for client adaptation.

---

## 1. IF Architecture

**Protocol:** 16:8 intermittent fasting, early eating window

**Window:** 8:00am – 2:00pm (6-hour practical eating window within 8-hour IF framework)

**Why the early window is metabolically superior for recomp:**
Insulin sensitivity follows a circadian rhythm — it peaks in the morning and declines through the afternoon and evening. Eating in alignment with peak insulin sensitivity means consumed carbohydrates are more efficiently partitioned into muscle glycogen rather than stored as fat. Evening eating elevates insulin during the lowest-sensitivity window, creating a less favorable partitioning environment for recomposition. Additionally, cortisol peaks naturally in the early morning (cortisol awakening response) — having the eating window open by 8am allows the first meal to coincide with the post-workout cortisol decline, supporting muscle protein synthesis rather than competing with it. Fasting overnight and extending it slightly into the morning also supports growth hormone pulsing, which occurs primarily during sleep and the early fasted period.

**The anchor rule:** The eating window is the non-negotiable anchor — not specific foods, not specific macros on any given day, not meal timing precision within the window. Imperfect food choices inside 8am–2pm still support the protocol. Perfect food choices outside the window do not.

**What breaks the fast:**
- Breaks the fast: any caloric intake, protein shakes, BCAAs in quantity sufficient to raise insulin measurably, food of any kind
- Does NOT break the fast (for fasting-state purposes): water, black coffee, plain matcha (no sweetener), electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium — zero calorie), pre-workout EAAs at low dose (the nuanced position: EAAs trigger a minor insulin response and technically provide calories, but the anabolic protection benefit for hard fasted training outweighs the minor fast disruption — this is a conscious tradeoff, not a mistake)

## 2. Daily Sequence Timeline

|------|--------|-------|

## 3. Pre-Workout Stack Breakdown

**Timing:** Taken ~6:00am, 30 minutes before training begins at 6:30am.

**Core principle:** The stack serves three functions in fasted training — anabolic protection (prevent muscle catabolism without breaking the fast), neuromuscular performance (focus, force output, endurance), and long-term structural support (gut health, hormonal buffering). Ingredients are sequenced so onset timing aligns with training demand.

---

**L-Tyrosine**
- **Role in fasted training:** Dopamine and norepinephrine precursor. In the fasted state, catecholamine availability can be limiting — tyrosine supplementation supports focus, motivation, and force output during early-morning fasted sessions.
- **Mechanism:** Converts to L-DOPA → dopamine → norepinephrine. Fasted state reduces competing amino acids in the bloodstream, improving CNS uptake.
- **Timing:** 30 min pre-training. Onset aligns with the start of the session.
- **Dose:** 500–2000mg. Start at 500mg, titrate upward.

---

**EAAs / BCAAs**
- **Role in fasted training:** Leucine-triggered muscle protein synthesis (MPS) and anabolic protection layer. Without EAAs, hard fasted training risks net muscle catabolism — cortisol is elevated, insulin is low, and muscle tissue is a target for gluconeogenesis.
- **Mechanism:** Leucine (the critical EAA) directly activates mTORC1 signaling independently of insulin. A leucine threshold of ~2-3g is sufficient to initiate MPS — achievable with 10-15g EAAs. BCAAs alone provide leucine but lack the full essential amino acid profile needed for complete MPS signaling.
- **Timing:** 20+ min before heavy lifting — EAAs require time to be absorbed and reach systemic circulation. Do not take immediately before training and expect protection.
- **Preference:** EAAs over BCAAs — the full essential amino acid profile provides more complete protection and better MPS signaling than leucine, isoleucine, and valine alone.

---

**Colostrum**
- **Role in fasted training:** Gut integrity protection and IGF-1 delivery. Hard training increases intestinal permeability — colostrum's lactoferrin, immunoglobulins, and growth factors support tight junction integrity. IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor) supports anabolic signaling independently of insulin.
- **Mechanism:** Bovine colostrum contains proline-rich polypeptides, immunoglobulins, and growth factors that resist gastric degradation and reach the intestinal epithelium. Fasted dosing is optimal — less competing food content allows greater bioavailability of active fractions.
- **Timing:** Take with pre-workout stack. Fasted state improves absorption.
- **Dose:** 10-20g bovine colostrum powder.

---

**Ginseng (Panax)**
- **Role in fasted training:** Adaptogen that blunts the cortisol spike associated with fasted high-intensity training. Secondary benefit is modest VO2 efficiency improvement via ginsenoside-mediated mitochondrial support.
- **Mechanism:** Ginsenosides modulate HPA axis activity, reducing the disproportionate cortisol response that can accompany early-morning fasted training. Does not suppress cortisol entirely — reduces the spike, not the baseline signal.
- **Timing:** 30 min pre-training. Consistent daily use amplifies adaptogenic effects.
- **Dose:** 200-400mg standardized extract (standardized to ginsenoside content).

---

**Matcha (taken separately at 5:45am)**
- **Role:** L-theanine + caffeine combination provides a longer, calmer alertness arc than straight caffeine. L-theanine modulates the stimulatory effect of caffeine, reducing jitteriness and extending the focus window.
- **Mechanism:** L-theanine promotes alpha brain wave activity, associated with calm focus. The caffeine-theanine ratio in matcha (~1:2 theanine:caffeine by effect) is distinct from coffee — lower caffeine content combined with L-theanine produces a different subjective experience.
- **Timing:** 30-45 min before the pre-workout stack (5:45am for a 6:00am stack). The longer caffeine arc from matcha provides sustained background alertness; the pre-workout stack hits on top of it. Staggering is intentional — simultaneous dosing produces a sharper but shorter spike.

---

**Creatine Monohydrate**
- **Role:** Phosphocreatine system support — increases PCr availability for high-intensity efforts, supports cellular hydration, and has modest neuroprotective properties.
- **Mechanism:** Saturates intramuscular creatine stores over 4-6 weeks of consistent daily dosing. No loading protocol required — 3-5g daily produces equivalent saturation over a longer timeline with less GI stress.
- **Timing:** Timing is not critical for creatine — take with pre-workout for convenience. Does not break the fast.
- **Dose:** 3-5g daily. No cycling required. Note: if not already in current stack, add — the evidence base is as strong as any supplement in existence.

---

**Timing summary for the full stack:** Matcha at 5:45am → full stack (tyrosine, EAAs, colostrum, ginseng, creatine) at 6:00am → training at 6:30am. EAAs reach bloodstream at approximately the time the first working set begins.

## 4. Day-Type Calorie and Macro Targets

**Protein anchor:** 200g every day, regardless of day type. Protein does not flex. Carbohydrates flex to match training demand. Fat fills remaining calories.

| Day | Session Type | Calories | Protein | Carbs | Note |
|-----|-------------|----------|---------|-------|------|
| Sunday | Heavy lower (deadlift focus) | 2700–2800 | 200g | 290–310g | Highest carb day — heavy loading + recovery runway for the week |
| Monday | Heavy upper (press/pull focus) | 2500–2700 | 200g | 250–280g | High carb — follows Sunday recovery, initiates weekly training block |
| Tuesday | Conditioning / Zone 2 | 2300 | 200g | 200–220g | Moderate carb — aerobic session doesn't deplete glycogen as aggressively |
| Wednesday | Moderate lower (squat + accessory) | 2500–2600 | 200g | 250–270g | Mid-week reload — refills glycogen for Thursday upper |
| Thursday | Moderate upper + arms | 2500–2700 | 200g | 250–280g | Matches Monday carb load — comparable volume and intensity |
| Friday | Mobility / light conditioning | 2300 | 200g | 200–220g | Low-load day — no need for full glycogen saturation |
| Saturday | Full rest or active recovery | 1750–1800 | 200g | 100–130g | Lowest carb day — no training stimulus to drive glycogen storage |
| Deload week (any day) | Reduced volume/intensity | 1800–2200 | 200g | 150–200g | Protein anchor held; carbs scaled to reduced demand |

**Key rules:**
- Protein floor is 200g — never drop below this regardless of calorie reduction
- On rest days, carb reduction comes primarily from eliminating starches at Meal 2 — protein and vegetable volume remain constant
- Fat intake adjusts passively — it fills the calorie gap between protein + carb totals and the day's calorie target
- Do not attempt to hit exact daily numbers — operate within the ranges; precision to ±50 calories is sufficient

## 5. Meal Templates

### Meal 1A — Recovery Shake

**Purpose:** Rapid post-training amino delivery and glycogen replenishment. Liquid form ensures gastric emptying and absorption speed that whole foods cannot match in the 30-minute post-training window.

**Macros:** ~45g protein | ~55g carbohydrates | ~440 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 1.5 scoops whey isolate (or blend) — 40–45g protein
- 1 medium banana (frozen preferred) — 27g carbs, potassium
- 100–150g frozen mixed berries — antioxidant load, 15–20g carbs
- 250ml water or unsweetened almond milk
- Optional: 5g creatine if not in pre-workout stack

**Prep time:** 3 minutes

---

### Meal 1B Option A — Egg and Oat Bowl

**Purpose:** Whole food follow-up meal 45–60 min after recovery shake. High fiber content supports satiety through the final hour of the eating window. Egg yolks provide dietary cholesterol supporting testosterone synthesis.

**Macros:** ~48g protein | ~65g carbohydrates | ~22g fiber | ~570 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 4 whole eggs + 2 egg whites, scrambled or soft-boiled
- 80g rolled oats (dry weight), cooked, topped with cinnamon
- 1 tbsp natural almond or peanut butter (stirs into oats)
- 100g blueberries or mixed berries
- Optional: psyllium husk 5g stirred into oats (adds 3–4g soluble fiber, improves LDL)

**Prep time:** 12 minutes

---

### Meal 1B Option B — Lentil and Sweet Potato Bowl (LDL-focus variant)

**Purpose:** Whole food meal designed to maximize soluble fiber for LDL management. Used on days when Meal 2 will be egg- or casein-heavy, or as a systematic swap for cardiovascular health objectives.

**Macros:** ~45g protein | ~60g carbohydrates | ~18g fiber | ~520 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 150g cooked green or black lentils (from Sunday prep)
- 150g roasted sweet potato (from Sunday prep)
- 4 egg whites or 60g Greek yogurt (protein addition)
- 1 tbsp tahini
- Handful of spinach or arugula
- Lemon juice, cumin, salt

**Prep time:** 5 minutes (assembly from Sunday prep)

---

### Meal 2 Option A — Lentil and Fish Bowl

**Purpose:** Closing anchor meal. High protein, high fiber, broad amino acid spectrum from combined plant and animal sources. Fish provides omega-3s for inflammation management and joint health.

**Macros:** ~88g protein | ~65g carbohydrates | ~20g fiber | ~900 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 180g salmon (baked) or 2 cans of skipjack tuna
- 200g cooked green lentils (from Sunday prep)
- 150g roasted sweet potato or 80g dry quinoa cooked
- Large handful of spinach or mixed greens
- 1–2 tbsp olive oil or tahini dressing
- Optional: 30g pumpkin seeds (adds 5g protein, zinc, magnesium)

**Prep time:** 5–8 minutes (assembly from Sunday prep; baking salmon adds 15 min if fresh)

---

### Meal 2 Option B — Casein and Edamame Bowl

**Purpose:** Closing anchor with slow-digesting protein to bridge the fasted period. Casein and edamame together provide a full amino acid spectrum and sustained release throughout the overnight fast. Preferred on rest days or when training the following morning at high intensity.

**Macros:** ~92g protein | ~60g carbohydrates | ~18g fiber | ~880 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 60g casein protein (mixed thick, pudding consistency with minimal water)
- 200g shelled edamame (frozen, thawed)
- 80g cooked quinoa
- 100g cottage cheese (extra leucine, texture)
- 1 tbsp almond butter or mixed nuts
- Optional: cinnamon, vanilla extract in the casein for palatability

**Prep time:** 5 minutes

---

### Meal 2 Travel — Portable Closing Anchor

**Purpose:** Maintains window-close protein floor and fiber load when no kitchen is available. Designed to be assembled from hotel pantry staples, airport options, and travel bag supplies.

**Macros:** ~75g protein | ~50g carbohydrates | ~15g fiber | ~740 calories

**Ingredients:**
- 2 scoops casein protein (pre-portioned bag from travel kit) + water
- 1 packet psyllium husk (from travel kit) stirred into casein
- 1 apple or banana (grabbed at hotel breakfast, airport, or convenience store)
- 1–2 packets Justin's almond butter or equivalent single-serve packet
- Optional: jerky (30–40g protein per 100g, non-perishable) if casein alone is insufficient

**Prep time:** 3 minutes (mix casein, eat banana, dip apple in almond butter)

## 6. Sunday Meal Prep System

**Total time:** 60–75 minutes
**Output:** 4–5 days of Meal 2 bases, 3–4 days of Meal 1B bases, travel bag restocked

---

**Block 1 — 0:00 to 0:05 (Setup + Start Long-Cook Items)**
- Start lentils: 200g dry green or black lentils + 600ml water, bring to boil then reduce. 20 min cook time.
- Start oats: 240g dry rolled oats + water, bring to boil, reduce, cover. 10 min cook time, cool before portioning.
- Start quinoa: 160g dry quinoa + 320ml water, bring to boil, reduce, cover. 20 min cook time.
- Preheat oven to 400°F (200°C).

**Block 2 — 0:05 to 0:20 (Oven Items — Active Time 15 min, Passive 25 min)**
- Cut 4–5 medium sweet potatoes into 3cm cubes. Toss with olive oil, salt, cumin, paprika.
- Cut broccoli, zucchini, or Brussels sprouts as available.
- Spread in single layer on two sheet pans.
- Into oven — 25–30 min. Stir once at the 15-minute mark.

**Block 3 — 0:20 to 0:35 (Protein Prep)**
- If using salmon: season 3–4 fillets, place in oven on a third pan for final 15–20 min of oven time.
- If using canned tuna: open and drain 6–8 cans, portion into containers (2 per Meal 2 container).
- Greek yogurt: portion 150g each into 3–4 small containers. Label and refrigerate.
- Protein powder: pre-measure 1.5 scoops into 5 small ziplock bags (3 for recovery shakes this week, 2 for travel bag). Seal and label.

**Block 4 — 0:35 to 0:50 (Containerize)**
- Meal 2 bases (4 containers): equal portions of lentils + quinoa or sweet potato + protein. Do not dress or add olive oil until serving — prevents sogginess.
- Meal 1B bases (3 containers): equal portions of cooked oats, stored plain. Blueberries portioned separately to avoid bleeding color into oats.
- Label all containers with day designation (Mon, Tue, Wed, Thu) if helpful for macro targets.

**Block 5 — 0:50 to 1:05 (Travel Prep + Fridge Organization)**
- Travel bags: restock 5 pre-workout bags, 3–5 protein bags, psyllium sachets x5, electrolyte packets x7.
- Fridge organization: proteins on top shelf, carb/veg bases on middle, Greek yogurt + berries front-center for visibility.
- Sticky note on fridge for mid-week restock: Greek yogurt + bananas + berries only. Everything else holds from Sunday.

**Mid-week restock rule:** Greek yogurt, fresh bananas, and berries are the only items that require a mid-week grocery run. All bases — lentils, oats, quinoa, sweet potato, baked protein — hold adequately refrigerated for 5 days. Do not complicate this.

## 7. Travel Nutrition Protocol

**Core principle:** The window is the anchor, not the food. Imperfect food choices inside 8am–2pm still preserve the protocol's metabolic benefits. Compliance is defined by window adherence and protein floor — not macronutrient precision.

**What breaks the protocol while traveling:**
1. Eating outside the 8am–2pm window (the cardinal violation)
2. Skipping the pre-workout stack on training days
3. Meal 1 protein below 60g (insufficient MPS trigger post-training)

**Non-negotiables in the travel bag:**

| Item | Quantity | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| Protein powder (pre-portioned bags) | 3–5 bags | Recovery shakes and window-close meals when food access is limited |
| Pre-workout bags (pre-portioned) | 5 bags | Full fasted training stack — do not leave these behind |
| Psyllium husk sachets | 5+ | Fiber supplement for hotel and restaurant food (hotel meals are universally low fiber) |
| Electrolyte packets | 7+ | Flying dehydrates aggressively; electrolytes also support training performance |
| Matcha single-serve packets | 5 | Maintains morning stack timing without needing a kitchen |
| Magnesium glycinate (200–400mg) | 7+ doses | Before bed on travel nights — blunts elevated cortisol from travel stress and time zone disruption, improves sleep onset and quality |

**Travel day execution:**
- Morning matcha from travel packets using hot water from hotel coffee maker or room service
- Pre-workout stack from pre-portioned bags + hotel room water
- Post-training: protein shake as Meal 1A, followed by hotel breakfast items (eggs, oatmeal, fruit) as Meal 1B within the window
- Meal 2: room service or restaurant protein-forward option, supplemented with casein bag if protein falls short, psyllium stirred into water alongside

**Time zone adjustment:** If travel shifts waking time by 2+ hours, shift the window by the same amount for the first 1–2 days before normalizing back to 8am–2pm. The window's biological function depends on consistency with the circadian rhythm, not on a fixed clock time.

## 8. Ab Vacuum — Fasted Practice Protocol

**Placement rationale:** The ab vacuum belongs in the nutrition system document because it is a fasted-state physiological practice, not a resistance training drill. Its efficacy depends entirely on performing it in the fasted morning state before any food, water, or supplement intake. It is an extension of the overnight fast, not a warm-up.

**Timing:** Immediately upon waking, before matcha, before water, before leaving bed.

**Mechanism:** The ab vacuum trains the transverse abdominis (TVA) — the deepest abdominal layer that acts as a natural "weight belt" for the spine. TVA activation also has a direct structural relationship to pull-up mechanics: a braced, compressed TVA creates full-body tension that improves lat recruitment and reduces energy leakage through the mid-section during pulling movements. In the fasted state, the abdominal cavity is decompressed (no food bulk, no gastric bloating), allowing maximal diaphragmatic excursion and the greatest possible TVA compression range.

**Execution:**

Step 1: Exhale completely. Expel all air from the lungs.
Step 2: Without inhaling, draw the navel toward the spine and upward. The goal is maximum hollowing of the lower abdomen — not just sucking in, but actively pulling the TVA inward and upward.
Step 3: Hold the compression. Breathe shallowly from the upper chest only (not diaphragmatic breathing, which would release the vacuum).
Step 4: Release fully on an inhale.

**Position progression by week:**

| Week | Position | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 1–2 | Supine (lying flat) | Lowest mechanical difficulty — gravity assists the hollow |
| 3–4 | Tabletop / quadruped | Gravity now works against the hollow; more TVA activation required |
| 5–6 | Seated upright | No positional support; full postural challenge |
| 7+ | Standing | Maximum difficulty; full postural load against gravity |

**Hold progression:**

| Week | Target hold | Sets |
|------|-------------|------|
| 1 | 10–15 seconds | 3 |
| 2–3 | 20–30 seconds | 3–4 |
| 4–5 | 40–50 seconds | 3–5 |
| 6+ | 60 seconds | 5 |

**Connection to pull-up mechanics:** Braced TVA creates the rigid mid-section that allows force to transfer between the lats and the arms without energy dissipation. Clients who lack this bracing typically exhibit hip sway, kipped form, or inability to complete the last inch of range at the top of the pull-up. Improving TVA activation via daily vacuum practice has a measurable carryover to pull-up rep quality within 4–6 weeks. This is the connection — it is not incidental.

## 10. Parametric Variables — Client Config Generation

This section defines what is fixed (protocol-level, applicable to most clients) vs. what is variable (individualized per client intake).

### Fixed Elements — Carry Forward to All Clients

| Element | Fixed Rule |
|---------|-----------|
| Protein anchor principle | Protein target does not flex regardless of calorie reduction. Cut carbs and fat; protect protein. |
| IF window concept | Early eating window aligned to training schedule and circadian rhythm. Window is the anchor, not specific foods or macros. |
| Sunday prep architecture | Batch cooking 1x/week for 4–5 days of bases. Mid-week restock of perishables only. |
| Pre-workout stack structure | EAAs + adaptogens + gut support. Timing: stack 30 min pre-training, matcha 30–45 min before stack. |
| Meal 1A = liquid recovery within 30 min post-training | Non-negotiable timing for the first post-training meal. |
| Protein threshold per meal | Minimum 30–40g protein per meal to reliably cross leucine threshold for MPS. |
| Fiber target | 30–40g/day minimum for training clients. Achieved through lentils, oats, psyllium, vegetables. |

### Variable Elements — Individualize Per Client

| Variable | Individualization Rule |
|----------|----------------------|
| Calorie targets | Based on bodyweight, activity level, and phase goal (recomp / cut / build). Start at 14–16 cal/lb for recomp; adjust at 2-week intervals based on weight trend and performance. |
| Carbohydrate targets | Scale to training volume and session type. High-volume strength days: 3–4g carbs/lb LBM. Low-intensity or rest days: 1–1.5g carbs/lb LBM. |
| Protein target | 0.8–1.0g per lb of bodyweight as a floor. For recomp clients in a calorie deficit, push toward 1.0–1.1g/lb to preserve lean mass. |
| IF window timing | Anchored to training time, not a fixed clock. Training at 6:30am → window opens at 8:00am. Training at 9:00am → window opens at 10:30am. Rest day windows can flex 1–2 hours without significant impact. |
| Meal template selections | Based on client food preferences, dietary restrictions, and LDL/cardiometabolic flags. Lentil-heavy templates for clients with elevated LDL. Egg-heavy for clients needing dietary cholesterol support. Travel template for clients with high travel frequency (>4 nights/month). |
| Pre-workout stack adjustments | Remove or replace ingredients based on tolerance (e.g., clients sensitive to tyrosine; clients who don't respond to ginseng; clients already getting colostrum from food sources). Creatine is the last thing to remove — evidence base is too strong to deprioritize. |
| Deload calorie target | Calculated at 70–80% of training day average. Protein anchor held. Carbs reduced proportionally to volume reduction. |

### Client Config Inputs Required (Intake Data)

To generate a client-specific config from this template, collect the following:

1. Bodyweight (lbs) and lean body mass estimate (or body fat %)
2. Training schedule: days/week, session type by day, typical session start time
3. Phase goal: recomp / fat loss / muscle gain / maintenance
4. Dietary restrictions or allergies
5. Travel frequency: average nights away per month
6. LDL / cardiovascular health flag (if known or relevant)
7. Current supplement stack (to avoid redundancy or conflict)
8. Food preferences and aversions within the template categories
9. Cooking access: full kitchen / partial / hotel-only / none
10. Window flexibility: does the client's schedule allow an early window, or does a late training time require a shifted window?
